    Identity During Apertheid

    If I could give Born a Crime a new title, it would beat“ Identity During Apartheid”. The word “Identity” describes finding out who you are. ”Apartheid was a system of racial segregation enforced by the white minority government in South Africa from 1948 to 1994. The reason that “Identity During Apartheid” would be the title of the book is because of apartheid it was difficult for Trevor to have an answer to “who am I?”. He was born to a white father, Robert, and his mother, Patricia, which didn’t fit into the government's laws. The book shows his struggle with this. The word “Apartheid” puts the story in a specific time and place, so readers can easily know what kind of challenges Trevor faced.

    Noyumbiselo's Legacy

    From the shadows of a forbidden birth to the global stage we all know, one name echoes with the strength, resilience, and unwavering love that shaped a comedic genius: Patricia Nombuyiselo. You know Trevor Noah as the sharp-witted host who effortlessly navigates cultural nuances and political satire. You might know his incredible story of growing up in apartheid South Africa, a child born into a world that deemed his very existence a crime. But at the heart of this extraordinary journey lies the enduring legacy of his mother, Nombuyiselo.

    “For me, becoming isn’t about arriving somewhere or achieving a certain aim. I see it instead as forward motion, a means of evolving, a way to reach continuously toward a better self. The journey doesn’t end.”
